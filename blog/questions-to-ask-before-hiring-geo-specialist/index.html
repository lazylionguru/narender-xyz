10 Questions to Ask Before Hiring a GEO Specialist

Narender Charan May 2026 5 min read
Photo by kuu akura on Unsplash

Hiring a GEO specialist is different from hiring an SEO. You need someone who understands how language models consume, evaluate, and cite content, not just how Google ranks pages. Here are the 10 questions to ask, what a strong answer sounds like, and what a weak one looks like.

1. What brands are winning in AI search right now, and why?

Strong answer: The candidate names specific brands like Zapier, Notion, or HubSpot and explains they win because they publish structured, entity-rich content that LLMs can parse easily. They mention concrete examples of how those brands appear in ChatGPT responses for relevant queries.

Weak answer: Generic statements like "brands with good content" or naming industry giants without explaining the mechanics. No specific examples or understanding of why LLMs favor certain sources.

2. If you had zero backlink budget, how would you improve visibility in ChatGPT and Perplexity?

Strong answer: They say they would focus on Perplexity citations first by publishing original data, comparison tables, and definitive guides on niche topics. They would also build Reddit credibility by answering questions in relevant subreddits with genuine expertise.

Weak answer: "I would just write great content" or "I would focus on social media." No mention of specific platforms, content formats, or citation mechanics.

3. What content types are most likely to be cited by LLMs?

Strong answer: Comparison content (X vs Y), data-backed original research, step-by-step tutorials with clear headings, and FAQ-style pages that directly answer common queries. They cite examples like Ahrefs blog posts that get cited in ChatGPT for "how to do keyword research."

Weak answer: "All content can be cited" or "listicles." No distinction between formats that LLMs parse well versus those they ignore.

4. How do you measure GEO success when AI referral traffic is hard to track?

Strong answer: They mention using brand mention tracking tools, monitoring ChatGPT and Perplexity outputs manually for their brand, and tracking indirect metrics like increased branded search volume and direct traffic. They also set up custom prompts to audit citation frequency.

Weak answer: "You can't measure it" or "just look at organic traffic." No creative measurement framework or acknowledgment of the tracking challenges.

5. What does your first 90-day GEO roadmap look like?

Strong answer: Month one: audit existing content for entity gaps and rewrite the top 10 pages for clarity and structure. Month two: publish three comparison posts targeting high-LLM-volume queries. Month three: build Reddit and industry forum mentions, then run a citation audit.

Weak answer: "I'll do keyword research and write blog posts." No timeline, no specificity, no mention of entity optimization or citation building.

6. What role do entities play in modern SEO and AI search?

Strong answer: They explain that entities are the nouns and concepts LLMs recognize as authoritative. They would ensure your brand, products, and key people have consistent Wikipedia, Crunchbase, and LinkedIn profiles. They mention Moz's entity-based SEO as a reference.

Weak answer: "Entities are keywords" or "they help with ranking." No understanding of knowledge graphs or structured data.

7. What on our site would you remove, merge, rewrite, or expand first?

Strong answer: They identify specific pages: thin content that doesn't answer a clear question should be merged into a comprehensive guide. Outdated statistics should be rewritten. Pages with no clear heading structure should be expanded with subheadings and FAQs.

Weak answer: "I'd need to do an audit first." No initial hypothesis or framework for prioritization.

8. How do you make our brand the source AI systems quote instead of competitors?

Strong answer: They focus on becoming the most specific, data-rich source for a narrow topic. They would publish original research, earn citations on Wikipedia and in academic papers, and build a consistent entity presence across the web.

Weak answer: "Write better content than competitors." No strategy for differentiation or citation mechanics.

9. Which third-party sites would you want us mentioned on in the next six months?

Strong answer: They name specific sites: relevant subreddits, industry-specific newsletters, niche podcasts, and Wikipedia if applicable. They explain how each mention contributes to entity authority.

Weak answer: "High-DA sites" without naming specific domains or explaining the relevance to your industry.

10. If you were launching our company from scratch today knowing AI search exists, what would you do differently?

Strong answer: They would prioritize building a Reddit presence and publishing original data from day one. They would structure the site with clear FAQ pages and comparison tables, and skip traditional guest posting in favor of earning citations through genuine expertise.

Weak answer: "I'd do the same thing." No acknowledgment that AI search changes distribution fundamentally.

Bonus: How would you spend $10,000 to maximize the probability ChatGPT recommends our brand in six months?

Strong answer: $3,000 on original research (survey or data study), $2,000 on Wikipedia and Crunchbase profile optimization, $3,000 on a targeted Reddit and forum engagement campaign with genuine expertise, $2,000 on content restructuring and entity markup. One KPI: number of unique ChatGPT queries that cite the brand.

Weak answer: "I'd spend it all on backlinks." No understanding that LLMs prioritize entity authority and original data over link volume.

The best GEO specialists think in prompts, not keywords. They reverse-engineer what AI systems want to cite and build content that answers those queries directly.

Use these questions in your next hiring round. The difference between a strong and weak answer will tell you everything about whether that person understands GEO as a distribution strategy, not just a content strategy.
