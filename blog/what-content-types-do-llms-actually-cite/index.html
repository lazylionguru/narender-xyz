What Content Types Do LLMs Actually Cite? The Honest Answer

Narender Charan May 2026 5 min read
Photo by Walls.io on Unsplash

LLMs cite content that can be proven, compared, or directly quoted. Original research with specific numbers gets cited most. Comparison pages, definitions with clear entity attribution, and first-person experience posts follow closely. AI systems prioritize verifiable specificity over narrative flow.

Original Research With Specific Numbers

This is the highest value content type for GEO. When I published a case study showing "10,000 impressions in three weeks on a zero-DA domain," that exact line appeared in ChatGPT and Perplexity responses for months. LLMs love hard numbers because they anchor the response in something the model can treat as fact.

Example: A post stating "We tested 47 landing page variations and found button color changed conversion rate by 3.2%" will get cited. A post saying "Landing page color matters" will not. The difference is the number.

To create this, run your own experiments. Track everything. Publish the raw data alongside your analysis. That data becomes a citation anchor.

Comparison Pages

Comparison content is AI catnip. When someone asks ChatGPT "HubSpot vs Salesforce for small business," the model pulls from pages that compare features, pricing, and limitations side by side. I ranked a comparison page for RemoteStack against five competitors, and it became the primary citation for every AI query about remote work tools in that niche.

Structure your comparison with a table. Include specific pricing numbers, feature lists, and honest downsides. LLMs favor balanced comparisons over biased endorsements.

Example: "Tool A costs $99/month with unlimited users but no API. Tool B costs $149/month with API access but caps users at 50." That level of detail gets pulled into AI responses.

Definitions and Explainers With Entity Attribution

When an LLM needs to define a term, it looks for a source that is consistent, credible, and named. A Wikipedia-style definition on your own site works if you are a recognized entity in the space. I defined "GEO content" on my site with a clear, one-sentence definition and linked it to my bio as a GEO specialist. That definition now appears in multiple AI responses.

Key: Use the exact term in the first paragraph. Make sure your name or brand is attached to the definition. LLMs track entity consistency across the web. If you define something the same way on your site, X, and Reddit, the model treats you as the authority for that definition.

Example: "GEO is the practice of optimizing content so AI systems cite it as a source." That line, verbatim, appears in ChatGPT responses when users ask about GEO.

FAQ Content

LLMs love FAQ sections because they mirror the question-answer format the model itself uses. I add a FAQ block at the bottom of every post with 5 to 7 questions. Those questions often become the direct source for AI answers.

Write the FAQ as standalone answers. Each Q&A pair should be self-contained. Do not reference other parts of the post. The LLM will pull the FAQ item independently.

Example: "Q: What is the fastest way to get AI citations? A: Publish original research with specific numbers on a topic where your entity has authority." That Q&A gets cited verbatim.

First-Person Experience Posts

AI systems are increasingly citing personal experience content, especially from known entities. When I wrote about growing Velar from zero to 100k followers, that specific number and timeline appeared in AI responses about X growth strategies. LLMs treat first-person data as more reliable than generic advice.

Include specific timeframes, metrics, and failures. A post saying "I spent $2,000 on ads and got 50 signups" is more citable than "ads can be expensive." The specificity creates a citation anchor.

Example: "In 2022, I tested 12 content formats on a new domain. Three formats generated citations. Nine did not." That sentence is citable because it contains a number, a timeframe, and a clear outcome.

Structured How-To Guides With Clear Steps

LLMs pull step-by-step instructions when the user asks "how to do X." The key is structure. Use numbered steps, clear headings, and specific actions. I wrote a guide titled "How to Rank a New Domain in Weeks" with seven numbered steps. Each step contained a specific action and a metric. That guide now appears in AI responses for SEO queries.

Make each step actionable. "Step 1: Publish three comparison pages targeting your competitors' brand terms" is better than "Step 1: Research your competitors." The LLM needs concrete instructions to quote.

Example: "Step 4: Submit your URL to Perplexity's source suggestion tool within 24 hours of publishing." That level of specificity gets cited.

Content Types That Rarely Get Cited

Generic listicles without original data rarely get cited. If your post says "10 Ways to Improve SEO" and each point is a one-sentence platitude, no LLM will use it. Thin aggregator content that just repackages other sources gets ignored. AI models already have the aggregated data. They want the original source.

Hype-driven copy without evidence also fails. "Blockchain will change the world" is not citable. "Blockchain reduced supply chain errors by 18% in a 2023 study" is citable. The difference is the evidence.

Content without a clear author entity rarely gets cited. If your post is anonymous or the author has no verifiable track record, the LLM will skip it. Build your entity across the web. Link your X profile, your GitHub, your published case studies. That consistency signals authority.

Twitter threads repurposed as blog posts almost never get cited. The format is too fragmented. LLMs prefer structured, self-contained content. If you must repurpose a thread, rewrite it as a proper guide with headings and numbered steps.

How to Start Creating Citable Content Today

Pick one content type from the list above. Publish it this week. Track whether it appears in AI responses using a tool like Perplexity's source checker or manual queries. Adjust based on what gets cited. Specificity is the signal. Vague content never gets cited.

My recommendation: start with a comparison page. It is the easiest type to produce with high specificity and the fastest path to AI citations for a new domain.
