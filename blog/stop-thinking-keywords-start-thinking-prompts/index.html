Stop Thinking in Keywords. Start Thinking in Prompts.

Narender Charan May 2026 6 min read
Photo by Levart_Photographer on Unsplash

The fundamental mindset shift for GEO is simple: stop thinking in keywords and start thinking in prompts. Keywords are what people type into Google. Prompts are what people ask AI. They are longer, more conversational, and more intent-specific. "best seo tool" is a keyword. "what SEO tool should I use if I am a solo founder with no budget" is a prompt. Optimising for the keyword gets you a ranking. Optimising for the prompt gets you a citation.

I learned this the hard way. In 2023, I ranked RemoteStack at number one for its core keyword on a new domain with zero backlinks. That was SEO. But when I started tracking citations in ChatGPT and Perplexity, I realised the same content was getting zero mentions. The AI wasn't pulling my answer because I wasn't answering the question the way people actually asked it.

So I rewrote the entire strategy. I stopped asking "what keyword has volume?" and started asking "what does a buyer actually say when they open ChatGPT?" The difference was night and day. Within weeks, I earned organic citations in ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini. No backlinks. No PR. Just content structured around prompts.

Why Prompts Beat Keywords

Keywords are short, fragmented, and often ambiguous. "SEO tools" could mean anything from a free meta tag checker to an enterprise rank tracker. Prompts contain context. They contain budget, role, pain point, and desired outcome. That context is exactly what an AI model needs to decide whether to cite your content.

When a user asks "what SEO tool should I use if I am a solo founder with no budget," the AI doesn't just look for the phrase "SEO tools." It looks for content that addresses budget constraints, solo founder workflows, and free or low-cost options. If your article covers all three, you win the citation.

Prompts are the new keywords. Specificity is the signal. Vague content never gets cited.

How to Research Prompts

You don't need expensive tools. Open ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Gemini, and ask it a question about your topic. Then look at the follow-up suggestions the AI provides. Those suggestions are real prompts people are using. Write them down.

Next, go to Reddit. Search your niche for posts that are phrased as questions. "How do I start SEO for a new blog?" "What tool do you use to track keywords?" These are prompts. Reddit is credibility, not a backlink channel. Every question on Reddit is a prompt you can reverse-engineer.

Finally, use AnswerThePublic or Google's "People also ask" section. But filter for long-tail phrases that sound like a conversation, not a search query. If it reads like something you would say to a colleague, it is a prompt.

Structuring Content for Prompt Intent

Once you have a list of prompts, do not write a generic article. Write a specific answer to one prompt per section. If the prompt is "what SEO tool should I use if I am a solo founder with no budget," your section heading should be exactly that question. Then answer it directly in the first sentence.

This is where most content fails. Writers use clever headings like "Budget-Friendly SEO Solutions for Solopreneurs." That sounds good to a human. But an AI model pulling a summary reads the heading and the first paragraph. If the heading does not match the prompt, the AI skips it.

Rewrite Your Titles and Intros

Your title should mirror the prompt. "Best SEO Tool for Solo Founders on a Zero Budget" is better than "Top SEO Tools 2025." The first one matches the question. The second one is generic and gets ignored by AI models.

Your intro should be the answer. First sentence: "If you are a solo founder with no budget, use Ubersuggest or SEO Minion." That is citable. An AI can pull that sentence and cite your article. If your intro is three paragraphs of context about the history of SEO, you lose the citation.

FAQ Sections Are Gold

FAQ sections are the easiest way to capture prompt traffic. Each FAQ item should be a complete prompt, not a short phrase. "How do I track keyword rankings for free?" not "Free keyword tracking." Write the answer in one to two sentences. Keep it direct.

I structure my FAQ sections with the exact prompts I found on Reddit and from AI follow-up suggestions. That section alone generates 30% of my GEO citations. It is low effort, high impact.

Why This Changes Everything

SEO is the foundation. GEO is the building on top. Without SEO, you have no traffic. Without GEO, you have no citations. But the mindset shift changes how you write every single piece of content.

When you think in prompts, you stop writing for the algorithm and start writing for the answer. You stop chasing keyword volume and start chasing specificity. You stop optimising for rankings and start optimising for citations. The result is content that works in both worlds.

I grew Velar's X account from zero to 100k followers by applying the same principle. Every post answered a specific question a crypto degen would ask. No fluff. No vague statements. Just direct answers that got cited and shared.

Actionable Steps You Can Take Today

First, open ChatGPT and ask it three questions about your niche. Write down the follow-up suggestions. Those are your prompts.

Second, go to Reddit and find five threads where someone asks a question about your topic. Copy the exact phrasing of the post title or the top comment's question.

Third, rewrite your next article so that every H2 matches one of those prompts. Answer the question in the first sentence of each section. No preamble.

Fourth, add an FAQ section with five to ten full-sentence prompts and one-sentence answers. That section alone will drive citations.

Fifth, publish and wait. You do not need backlinks for GEO. You need specificity. Backlinks are grunt work, not budget work. Spend your time on prompt research instead.

Stop thinking in keywords. Start thinking in prompts. That is the only mindset shift you need to win in GEO.
