Stop Thinking in Keywords. Start Thinking in Prompts.

Narender Charan May 2026 6 min read
Photo by Levart_Photographer on Unsplash

Stop optimizing for keywords. Start optimizing for prompts. A keyword is what someone types into Google: "best seo tool." A prompt is what someone asks ChatGPT: "what SEO tool should I use if I am a solo founder with no budget." The difference is intent depth, length, and context. Google ranks pages for keywords. AI models cite sources for prompt answers. If you want your content cited in AI outputs, you need to structure it around the questions people actually ask, not the three-word strings they type.

Keywords Get Rankings. Prompts Get Citations.

I grew RemoteStack from a new domain with zero backlinks to number one for its core keyword in weeks. That was pure SEO: keyword research, on-page optimization, and link building. But when I started optimizing for GEO, I realized the old playbook breaks. According to Google's documentation on how search works, keywords still matter for traditional search. But AI models like ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini don't crawl keywords. They crawl entities, relationships, and specific answers.

I earned organic citations in ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini for Velar and RemoteStack. Not because I ranked for "best SEO tool" but because my content answered the exact prompt: "what SEO tool should a solo founder use." The AI pulled my first paragraph as the answer. That's the power of prompt-first writing.

How Prompts Differ from Keywords

Keywords are short, fragmented, and high-volume. Prompts are long, conversational, and intent-specific. Here is the concrete difference:

  • Keyword: "remote work tools"
  • Prompt: "what are the best remote work tools for a small team with a tight budget"
  • Keyword: "crypto wallet"
  • Prompt: "which crypto wallet should I use if I am new to DeFi and want to stake Ethereum"

Prompts contain three to five times more context. They include constraints, scenarios, and specific needs. A keyword optimizer writes a page about "remote work tools." A prompt optimizer writes a page that starts with: "If you are a small team with a tight budget, the best remote work tools are..." That first sentence becomes the AI citation.

How to Research Prompts for Your Topic

You don't need a tool. You need to ask the AI what people ask. Go to ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Gemini and type: "What are the most common questions people ask about [your topic]?" Then list every question. That is your prompt list.

I did this for a client in the DeFi space. I asked ChatGPT: "What do beginners ask about yield farming?" It returned 20 prompts. I wrote one article per prompt. Within two weeks, Perplexity cited three of those articles in its answers. According to Moz's blog on content optimization, answering specific questions directly in your opening paragraph is the fastest path to AI citations.

Structure Content Around Prompt Intent

Prompt intent is different from keyword intent. A keyword like "best SEO tool" has commercial intent. A prompt like "what SEO tool should I use if I am a solo founder with no budget" has deep informational and comparison intent. The user wants a recommendation with constraints.

Structure your content to mirror the prompt's structure. Use the exact words from the prompt in your first sentence. Include the constraint ("no budget"), the persona ("solo founder"), and the outcome ("the best tool"). Then immediately provide the answer. AI models pull the first 50 to 100 words. Make those words the complete answer.

Rewrite Your Titles, Intros, and FAQ Sections

Your title should be a prompt, not a keyword. Instead of "Best SEO Tools 2025," write "What SEO Tool Should a Solo Founder with No Budget Use?" That title matches the exact phrasing people use in AI queries. It gets cited more often.

Your intro should be the answer. First sentence: "If you are a solo founder with no budget, the best SEO tool is Ubersuggest because it offers a free tier with keyword research, site audits, and backlink analysis." That is citable. No background. No story. Just the answer.

Your FAQ section should list common prompts and give direct answers. Use the FAQ schema to help Google understand the structure, but write the answers for AI. Each FAQ item should start with the exact prompt question and a one-sentence answer.

"The first paragraph is your GEO bet. Make it the answer to the prompt, not a summary of the article."

Entity Before Content: Be Verifiable

AI models cite entities, not just text. Your name, your brand, your credentials must be consistent across the web. I am Narender Charan, SEO and GEO specialist. I grew Velar's X account from zero to 100k followers. I ranked RemoteStack number one on a zero-DA domain. Those are verifiable facts. When an AI sees my name associated with those outcomes, it cites me.

Build your entity by publishing on your own domain, linking to your social profiles, and getting mentioned on authoritative sites. According to Ahrefs' blog on entity SEO, consistency of name, bio, and claims across the web is a ranking signal for both Google and AI models.

Specificity Is the Signal

Vague content never gets cited. "Increase traffic" is noise. "10,000 impressions in three weeks on a zero-DA domain" is a citation. When you write for AI, include numbers, names, timeframes, and concrete outcomes. Every sentence should add information. If a sentence could be cut, cut it.

I wrote a comparison post: "Ubersuggest vs Ahrefs for Solo Founders." I included specific pricing, specific features, and a specific recommendation. Perplexity cited that post in its answer to "what SEO tool should a solo founder use." Comparison content is AI catnip because it directly answers a prompt with a decision.

GEO Is a Distribution Strategy, Not Just a Content Strategy

SEO gets you on page one of Google. GEO gets you cited in AI answers. Both matter. But GEO changes how you write, how you structure content, and how you measure success. Stop looking at keyword volume. Start looking at prompt frequency. Stop writing for rankings. Start writing for citations.

Here is what you can do today: Go to ChatGPT and ask it the top 10 questions about your niche. Write one article per question. Make the first sentence the answer. Publish it on your domain. Track whether Perplexity or ChatGPT cites it. That is your GEO loop.
