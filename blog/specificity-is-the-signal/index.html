Specificity Is the Signal: Why Vague Content Never Gets Cited by AI

Narender Charan May 2026 5 min read
Photo by Campaign Creators on Unsplash

AI systems extract and cite specific, verifiable claims. They skip vague generalisations entirely. If your content says "increase traffic," it gets ignored. If it says "10,000 impressions in three weeks on a zero-DA domain," it becomes a citation in ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini.

Why Specificity Signals Authority to AI

Generative engines rank content based on trust signals. Specificity is the strongest trust signal because it is verifiable. A vague claim like "our strategy improved rankings" cannot be checked. A specific claim like "ranked RemoteStack number one for its core keyword in six weeks" can be cross-referenced across the web.

AI models learn to associate precision with credibility. When you include a number, a name, or a timeframe, you give the model a hook to cite you as the source for that fact. Vague content offers no hooks. It gets summarised by the AI and attributed to no one.

Entity recognition is key here. Google and AI systems build knowledge graphs of entities: people, companies, products, metrics. Every specific claim strengthens your entity. Every vague sentence weakens it.

“Specificity is the difference between being a source and being noise.”

How to Audit Your Existing Content for Vague Claims

Open your last five posts. Scan every sentence for words like "improve," "increase," "optimise," "grow," "better," "effective." These are vague verbs that trigger AI to skip your paragraph.

Replace each vague verb with a concrete outcome. If you cannot attach a number, a name, or a timeframe to the claim, delete the sentence or rewrite it.

Vague vs Specific: Before and After

Here is the difference between content that gets ignored and content that gets cited.

Vague: "Our content strategy helped a client grow their organic traffic."
Specific: "Grew Velar's X account from zero to 100,000 followers in 12 months using daily thread-based content."

Vague: "We improved search rankings for competitive keywords."
Specific: "Ranked RemoteStack at number one for 'remote developer hiring' in six weeks on a new domain with zero backlinks."

Vague: "The tool saves time for content teams."
Specific: "Reduced content production time from 8 hours to 45 minutes per piece for a team of five writers."

Vague: "We earned mentions in major AI platforms."
Specific: "Earned organic citations in ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini without any outreach or paid placements."

How to Build a Habit of Writing Specific Content

This is not a one-time audit. It is a writing discipline. Start with these three rules.

1. Lead with the Number

Write the number first, then the context. "10,000 impressions in three weeks" beats "We saw a significant increase in impressions." Open every paragraph with the most specific fact you have.

If you do not have the number yet, do not publish. Collect the data first. Run the test. Measure the outcome. Then write.

2. Name Names

AI systems love named entities. Use company names, tool names, platform names, and person names. "Grew Velar's X account" is stronger than "grew a client's social media." "Ranked RemoteStack" is stronger than "ranked a SaaS company."

If you cannot name the entity, anonymise with specific details. "A B2B SaaS with 50 employees and a zero-DA domain" is still specific enough to be cited.

3. Attach Timeframes

Every claim needs a timeframe. "In three weeks," "over six months," "within 48 hours." Timeframes tell the AI that your result is bounded and measurable. Without a timeframe, the claim is unverifiable.

Set a timer when you write. Spend 60 seconds adding a number, a name, or a timeframe to every sentence that currently lacks one.

Why Reddit Is the Best Training Ground for Specificity

Reddit punishes vague content. A post that says "my SEO strategy worked" gets downvoted. A post that says "used a single backlink from a .edu forum and hit 5,000 clicks in 10 days" gets upvoted and saved.

Write your next piece of content as if it will be posted to a competitive subreddit. Imagine the comments: "Source?" "Prove it." "Show the screenshots." If your content cannot survive that scrutiny, it will not survive an AI citation check.

The Entity Before Content Rule

Being specific is not enough if your entity is inconsistent. I am Narender Charan, SEO and GEO specialist, Web3 content strategist, crypto degen. That entity needs to be the same on my website, X, LinkedIn, and every guest post. If the AI sees "Narender Charan" on my site and "Narinder Charan" on a guest post, it loses trust in both.

Audit your entity consistency. Same name. Same photo. Same bio. Same tone. Same claims. Every place you appear should reinforce the same specific facts.

Actionable Next Step

Take one post you published in the last 30 days. Highlight every sentence that contains a vague claim. Rewrite each one with a specific number, name, or timeframe. Republish or update the post. Track whether your AI citation rate increases in the next 30 days.

That is the test. Not theory. Not speculation. A measurable outcome on a specific piece of content.
