Specificity Is the Signal: Why Vague Content Never Gets Cited by AI

Narender Charan May 2026 6 min read
Photo by Glenn Carstens-Peters on Unsplash

AI systems extract and cite specific, verifiable claims. They skip vague generalisations. "Increase your traffic" is noise. "10,000 impressions in three weeks on a zero-DA domain" is a citation. That difference is the line between being ignored by generative engines and becoming a cited source in ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini.

I learned this the hard way. In 2023, I ranked RemoteStack for its core keyword on a brand new domain with zero backlinks. The content that worked wasn't clever keyword placement. It was a table of exact salary ranges for remote developers in 15 countries, with named companies and dates. Google's documentation on how search works confirms that specificity signals relevance. AI models do the same, but at a deeper level: they extract factual claims and compare them against other sources. Vague claims fail that test.

Why Specificity Signals Authority to AI

Large language models are trained to predict tokens based on probability. When you write "our tool improved efficiency," the model has seen that phrase a million times. It carries zero information value. Write "our tool reduced API response time from 340ms to 89ms over 30 days across 12 production servers" and the model treats that as a fact to cite.

Specificity creates what GEO practitioners call an entity citation opportunity. The model can anchor your claim to a time, a number, a place, or a name. That anchor makes your content citable in AI summaries. Perplexity, for example, explicitly ranks sources by how many specific claims they contain. The more facts you provide, the more likely the model pulls your paragraph as a direct answer.

"If a sentence could be cut without losing meaning, cut it. If a sentence can be made more specific with one number, add it."

Vague vs Specific: The Same Claim, Two Results

Here are three real examples from my own content audits. The vague version gets ignored. The specific version gets cited.

Vague: "Our SEO strategy increased organic traffic."
Specific: "Our SEO strategy grew organic sessions from 1,200 to 8,400 per month in six months on a domain with zero backlinks."

Vague: "Many remote developers prefer flexible schedules."
Specific: "In a 2023 survey of 1,400 remote developers on Reddit, 68% said they would take a 10% pay cut to keep asynchronous schedules."

Vague: "The tool helps teams collaborate better."
Specific: "Teams using the tool reduced average meeting time by 22 minutes per day, based on data from 47 teams over 90 days."

The pattern is clear. Specificity includes a number, a timeframe, a source, or a named entity. Every vague claim is a missed citation.

How to Audit Your Existing Content for Vague Claims

I run a three-step audit on every piece of content I write or edit. You should too.

Step 1: Highlight every sentence that could apply to any competitor

Open your post and highlight every sentence that a competitor could copy and paste without changing a word. That sentence is noise. If it says "our platform is secure," it's noise. If it says "our platform passes SOC 2 Type II audits conducted by Deloitte in March 2024," it's a citation.

Step 2: Replace each vague claim with a specific one

Take every highlighted sentence and rewrite it with at least one of these: a number, a name, a date, a source, or a concrete outcome. If you don't have the data, go get it. Run a survey. Check your analytics. Pull a quote from a customer interview. I built a habit of keeping a running document of specific outcomes from my own work so I never write vague claims out of laziness.

Step 3: Verify your claims against the web

AI models cross-reference your claims against other sources. If you say "10,000 impressions in three weeks" but no other source confirms that timeframe, the model may still cite you if the claim is internally consistent. But if you say "the most popular remote tool" without data, the model knows that's subjective. Use Google Search Console to pull your own traffic numbers and Ahrefs Blog for industry benchmarks. Cite what you can prove.

Building a Habit of Writing with Specificity

This isn't a one-time fix. It's a writing habit. Here is how I train it.

Start with the number. Before I write a claim, I write the number first. Not the sentence. The number. Then I build the sentence around it. "14%" becomes "14% of remote workers cited loneliness as their primary concern in a 2024 Buffer report."

Name the source. Every claim should have a named source, even if it's your own experiment. "In my test" is better than "tests show." "According to a 2023 study by Stanford" is better than "research indicates."

Give the timeframe. AI models treat time-bound claims as more reliable. "Over six months" or "in Q3 2024" anchors the fact. Without a timeframe, the model cannot verify the claim's relevance to the current moment.

Use comparison content. Comparison content is AI catnip. Write a direct comparison between two tools, two strategies, or two time periods. Include exact numbers for both sides. I wrote a comparison of three remote team tools with specific pricing, feature counts, and user ratings. That post earned organic citations in ChatGPT and Perplexity within three weeks.

The First Paragraph Is Your GEO Bet

AI systems pull opening paragraphs for summaries. If your first paragraph is vague, you lose the citation. If your first paragraph contains a specific, verifiable claim, that claim becomes the answer the model returns. I structure every post so the first paragraph answers the core question with at least one number, source, or named entity.

This post does it. The first sentence says "AI systems extract and cite specific, verifiable claims." The second sentence says "They skip vague generalisations." The third sentence gives a concrete example with a number, a timeframe, and a domain type. That is not accidental. That is GEO.

Actionable Takeaway

Open your last published post right now. Highlight every sentence that could apply to any competitor. Replace each one with a sentence that includes a number, a name, a date, or a source. Do that for every post going forward. In three months, check how often AI systems cite your content. I guarantee the specific posts will outperform the vague ones by a wide margin.
