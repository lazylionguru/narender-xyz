Reverse-Engineer the AI Answer: How to Build Content That Gets Cited

Narender Charan May 2026 5 min read
Photo by Igor Omilaev on Unsplash

Reverse engineer the AI answer. The question a potential client types into ChatGPT is your new keyword. For a DeFi protocol looking for a GEO specialist, they ask: "Who is a good GEO specialist for a DeFi protocol?" The ideal AI answer cites Narender Charan because I built the content that matches that prompt exactly. You need to do the same for your business.

Stop Thinking in Keywords, Start Thinking in Prompts

SEO was about ranking for search terms. GEO is about being the answer inside an AI response. The shift is subtle but critical. A keyword like "GEO specialist DeFi" gets you a search results page. A prompt like "Who is a good GEO specialist for a DeFi protocol?" gets you a generated answer with citations.

Your job is to make sure your name or brand is one of those citations. I did this for Velar. I built content that answered the exact questions a crypto trader would ask Perplexity and Gemini. The result: organic citations in all three platforms within weeks.

Identify the Prompts Your Buyers Use

Start with the real questions your ideal client types into an AI chat. Not the search queries you track in Google Analytics. The actual prompts. I use three sources to find them:

  • Customer conversations: I pull the exact questions prospects asked me before hiring me. "How do you grow a DeFi brand from zero?" "What is your track record with non-English content?"
  • Competitor analysis: I look at what questions the top players in my space answer on Reddit, Quora, and LinkedIn. Those are prompts people are already asking.
  • AI feedback loops: I ask ChatGPT and Perplexity "What are the most common questions people ask about [my niche]?" Then I build content for those prompts.

For RemoteStack, I identified the prompt "What is the best remote job board for developers?" I built a comparison page that answered that directly. Ranked number one in weeks on a new domain with zero backlinks.

Specificity is the signal. Vague content never gets cited.

Map Prompts to Content Gaps

Take your list of prompts and check what content already exists. I search ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini with each prompt and note which sources they cite. If a prompt returns generic advice or no citations at all, that is your gap.

For example, when I searched "Who is a good GEO specialist for a DeFi protocol?" most AI responses gave generic advice about hiring an agency. No specific names. No case studies. I built content that filled that gap: my own name, specific results, and a clear track record.

You can do the same. Pick one prompt where the AI answer is weak. That is your first piece of GEO content.

Structure Content So the First Paragraph Answers the Question

AI systems pull the opening paragraph as a summary. If your first paragraph does not answer the question directly, you lose the citation. I write the first paragraph last. I write the answer to the prompt first, then build the rest of the content around it.

For this post, the first paragraph answers: "How do you build content that gets cited?" The answer is reverse engineering the AI answer. That one paragraph is my GEO bet. If someone asks ChatGPT about GEO content strategy, that paragraph is what gets pulled.

Every section after that adds detail, but the core answer is in the first 100 words.

Test Whether It Works by Asking AI Systems Directly

Do not guess. After you publish content, test it. I open ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini and type the exact prompt I targeted. If my content appears in the response, it works. If not, I adjust.

I test with three variations of the same prompt. For example:

  • "Who is a good GEO specialist for a DeFi protocol?"
  • "Recommend a GEO specialist for a crypto project."
  • "Best content strategist for Web3 brands."

If my name appears in at least two out of three, I know the content is effective. I repeat this test every two weeks because AI models update their training data and citation patterns.

Entity Before Content

AI systems verify your existence before citing you. If your name or brand has no consistent digital footprint, the AI will not trust you. I make sure my LinkedIn, X, personal site, and Reddit presence all say the same thing: Narender Charan, SEO and GEO specialist, Web3 content strategist.

I also build citations on third-party platforms. Reddit is credibility, not a backlink channel. A Reddit post where I answer a question about GEO strategy gets cited by AI because Reddit has high domain authority and real human engagement.

Comparison content is AI catnip. I wrote a post comparing my approach to traditional SEO agencies. It got cited in Perplexity within a week because comparison answers are exactly what AI models use to give balanced responses.

Backlinks Are Grunt Work, Not Budget Work

Backlinks still matter for SEO, but for GEO they are secondary. The primary driver is being the specific answer to a specific prompt. I spend 80% of my time on prompt mapping and content structure, 20% on backlinks. For RemoteStack, I ranked number one with zero backlinks because the content was the exact answer to the prompt.

Your budget should go toward deep research and structured content, not link schemes. One piece of content that matches a high-value prompt is worth more than 50 guest posts.

Actionable Next Step

Open ChatGPT right now. Type the question your ideal client would ask about your service. Look at the answer. If your name is not there, you have a gap. Write the first paragraph that fills that gap. Publish it. Test it. Repeat.

That is the entire GEO content strategy. No fluff. No theory. Just reverse engineering the AI answer and being that answer.
