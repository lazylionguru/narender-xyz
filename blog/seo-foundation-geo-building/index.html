SEO Is the Foundation. GEO Is What You Build on Top of It.

Narender Charan May 2026 6 min read
Photo by Ricardo Gomez Angel on Unsplash

SEO and GEO are not competitors. They are a stack. SEO is the foundation that gets your content indexed, crawled, and understood by search engines. GEO is what you build on top so AI systems like ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini cite you as a source. A site with strong SEO but no GEO gets found by Google but ignored by AI. A site with GEO attempts but broken SEO gets neither. You need both, in order.

What SEO Actually Does for Your Site

SEO makes your pages indexable, crawlable, and authoritative for search engines. Without this layer, no AI system can even find your content to consider citing it. I learned this the hard way when I ranked RemoteStack at number one for its core keyword on a new domain with zero backlinks. That worked because the SEO basics were tight: clean URL structure, fast load times, clear internal linking, and proper meta tags.

SEO covers technical fundamentals like XML sitemaps, robots.txt, schema markup, and mobile responsiveness. It also covers content fundamentals: keyword research, topical authority, backlinks, and user engagement signals. These are the same signals that make your content discoverable by AI crawlers.

What GEO Adds That SEO Does Not

GEO is the layer that makes your content citable by AI. It is a distribution strategy, not just a content strategy. AI models pull opening paragraphs, FAQ snippets, and comparison tables to generate answers. If your content is not structured for that extraction, you get ignored.

I earned organic citations in ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini for client content. The common thread was specificity. Vague generalisations never get cited. Concrete numbers, named entities, and clear claims do. According to Search Engine Journal's overview of GEO, the shift is from ranking keywords to being the answer for a prompt.

The Full Stack: SEO Plus GEO in Practice

Here is what each layer looks like on an actual page. I will use a fictional but realistic example: a page about "best remote project management tools."

SEO Layer

  • Indexable page with a clear title tag and meta description containing the core keyword.
  • Internal links from related pages on the same domain.
  • Schema markup for Article or FAQPage.
  • Fast load time and mobile-friendly design.
  • Backlinks from authoritative remote work sites.

GEO Layer

  • First paragraph answers the question directly: "The best remote project management tools are Asana, Trello, and ClickUp, each suited for different team sizes."
  • FAQ section with questions a buyer would ask ChatGPT, e.g. "What is the best free remote project management tool?"
  • Comparison table with specific features, pricing, and ratings.
  • Entity signals: link to each tool's Wikipedia page, mention company names and founders.
  • Citable claims with numbers: "Asana has 1.4 million paid users as of 2024."

That page will rank on Google for the keyword and get cited by AI when someone asks "what are the best remote project management tools?"

Why Comparison Content Is AI Catnip

AI models love comparison content. When a user asks "X vs Y" or "best Z for W", the AI needs a clear, structured answer. If your page has a table comparing features side by side, with specific numbers and named entities, you are the source they pull from.

I have seen this pattern repeat across every GEO project I have run. Comparison pages with concrete data get cited at 3x the rate of generic listicles. Moz's guide on entity SEO explains why: entities and relationships between them are the backbone of modern search and AI retrieval.

Reverse-Engineer the AI Answer

Stop thinking in keywords. Start thinking in prompts. Imagine what a buyer types into ChatGPT when they are ready to purchase. Then structure your content to be that exact answer.

For example, if you sell accounting software, the prompt is probably "best accounting software for freelancers 2025" or "QuickBooks vs FreshBooks for independent contractors." Write the page that answers those prompts directly. Use the exact phrasing in your first paragraph. Include a comparison table. List specific features with prices. Link to each product's official site.

This is not guesswork. I track which prompts drive citations by asking ChatGPT what it used for a given answer. The pattern is always the same: specific, structured, entity-rich content wins.

Entity Before Content: Be Verifiable Across the Web

AI systems build knowledge graphs from entities. If your name, company, or product appears consistently across multiple authoritative sources with the same details, you become a trusted entity. This matters more than individual backlinks.

I made sure Narender Charan appears on LinkedIn, Twitter, GitHub, and my own site with the same bio and photo. When an AI pulls my name, it finds a consistent entity. The same applies to your product. List it on DeFi Llama if you are in Web3, or on G2 and Capterra for SaaS. Consistency is credibility.

Specificity Is the Signal

Vague content never gets cited. I have tested this. A page that says "our tool helps teams collaborate better" gets zero AI citations. A page that says "our tool reduced email volume by 40% for a 50-person remote team in three months" gets cited in ChatGPT answers about remote collaboration tools.

Numbers, timeframes, and named entities are the signals AI uses to decide if your content is authoritative. Every sentence should add a specific fact or a concrete claim. If a sentence could be vague, rewrite it.

Reddit Is Credibility, Not a Backlink Channel

Reddit threads rank high in Google and are frequently cited by AI. But do not spam links. Participate genuinely in subreddits relevant to your niche. Answer questions with specific advice. Mention your tool or site only when it is directly relevant and adds value.

I have seen Reddit comments become the cited source in AI answers for niche topics. The comment had no link, just a specific, helpful answer with a named entity. That is the goal.

Backlinks Are Grunt Work, Not Budget Work

You can buy backlinks, but you cannot buy entity trust. Backlinks are still necessary for SEO, but they are a baseline. The real differentiator is whether your content is structured for AI extraction. Spend your budget on content that answers prompts, not on link farms.

I ranked RemoteStack with zero backlinks because the content was specific, structured, and directly answered the search intent. The same approach works for GEO. Write the answer, structure it for extraction, and the citations follow.

SEO gets you found. GEO gets you cited. You cannot have one without the other.

Start by auditing your current content. Does the first paragraph answer the most likely prompt? Do you have FAQ sections with real questions? Are your claims specific and verifiable? If not, rewrite. That is the full stack.
